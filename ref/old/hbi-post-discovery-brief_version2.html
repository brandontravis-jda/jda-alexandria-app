Section 01

Project Context

Heartbeat International's current website runs on Joomla with a CiviCRM database backend. The site is a combined instance — heartbeatinternational.org (donor/public-facing) and heartbeatservices.org (affiliate-facing) share the same Joomla installation, differentiated only by template assignment. There is no reliable way to distinguish which "site" a page belongs to on the backend until it's published.

The site is under active attack. In one 15-minute window observed by HBI's IT lead, 150,000 bot requests hit the server. Joomla's age prevents modern security features from being implemented. When bot traffic coincides with CiviCRM report generation, both sites go down completely. Cloudflare has been layered on as mitigation, but the underlying platform cannot support the protections HBI needs.

CiviCRM is being replaced by HubSpot. This is already in progress and is a separate workstream. The database migration removes the dependency that previously made the website rebuild feel too risky to attempt — HBI described it as a "domino effect" they were waiting to trigger.

This brief covers heartbeatinternational.org only. Heartbeatservices.org will be addressed in a separate discovery session.

The client has had conversations about this rebuild for at least six years. They used the phrase "blow it up" to describe their posture toward Joomla and CiviCRM. The appetite for a clean start is unambiguous.

Section 02

Audience Hierarchy

When design or content decisions create tension between these audiences, the higher-ranked audience wins.

Priority 1 — Primary
Donors (Current and Prospective)
Who they are: Individuals motivated by the injustice of attacks on life-affirming care, the tangible impact of Option Line and APRN, and the emotional resonance of stories from mothers whose lives were changed. They arrive via email campaigns, direct mail, lawsuit awareness, and organic search.
Job to be done: Understand what Heartbeat does, feel the impact, and convert to a financial gift — or enter the nurture pipeline by subscribing.
What they need to feel: Influential, active, visionary, passionate, confident. They need to see that their contribution creates immediate, measurable impact.
Primary action: Donate. Secondary action: Subscribe to the nurture list (email acquisition).
Priority 2 — Secondary
General Public and Information Seekers
Who they are: People who have heard about Heartbeat through news coverage, lawsuits, or word of mouth and want to learn about the pregnancy help movement — what a pregnancy center is, what Heartbeat does, how the network operates.
Job to be done: Get educated on Heartbeat's mission and the pregnancy help movement. Become a champion for pregnancy help in their community.
What they need to feel: Authentic, trustworthy, experienced, comprehensive, inspiring.
Primary action: Explore content, download a resource (email acquisition gate), or find a local pregnancy help organization via the Worldwide Directory.
Priority 3 — Tertiary
Churches and Pastors
Who they are: Faith leaders who could mobilize their congregations to support the pregnancy help movement. HBI has invested in a dedicated pastor's page that speaks to this audience differently and equips them with distinct resources.
Job to be done: Understand how to champion pregnancy help from the pulpit and connect their congregation to the mission.
What they need to feel: Respected as partners, equipped with practical tools, inspired to act.
Primary action: Engage with pastor-specific content, connect with Heartbeat.
Priority 4 — Tertiary
Media, Political, and Governmental Stakeholders
Who they are: Journalists, policymakers, and advocacy contacts seeking press releases, organizational credibility, and positioning statements.
Job to be done: Quickly access press materials, financials (legally required), and organizational bona fides.
Primary action: Access press releases, download media resources.

Audience NOT served on this site: Affiliates and network member organizations. They are actively redirected to heartbeatservices.org. Some land on heartbeatinternational.org by accident; the current site includes top-of-page messaging to redirect them. This pattern should be preserved and improved.

Audience requiring delicate treatment: Women in need of pregnancy help. Heartbeat International is not the direct service provider (that's Option Line and local affiliates), but visitors who arrive looking for help must be redirected compassionately and quickly. The current site does this; the new site must preserve and refine it.

Content built from the ground up for heartbeatinternational.org. Client No bulk content migration. Pages are authored fresh. CMS must support preview before publish. Client The current Joomla setup requires publishing a page to see it. The new CMS must provide a preview/staging capability. 3-4 team members publish content. Workflow: Google Doc → department lead approval → Comms team proofreads/layouts → publish → review.

Section 05

Content Strategy

Content That Drives Donor Action

Three content pillars emerged as the primary drivers of donor engagement and conversion:

Lawsuits and movement defense. Coverage of the California and New York AG lawsuits, along with past incidents of vandalism and attacks on pregnancy help organizations (2022), activates what HBI described as a "David vs. Goliath" response. Donors are motivated by perceived injustice against life-affirming care. This content needs to be current, accessible, and connected to a clear giving path.

Option Line impact. The 24/7 contact center's measurable outcomes — calls answered, women connected to help — give donors who want tangible, numbers-driven impact something concrete to support. Pairs data points with stories.

Abortion Pill Rescue Network milestones. The recent 8,000-lives-saved milestone is a major narrative anchor. APRN combines the emotional power of individual rescue stories with the scale of a growing movement. This content does double duty: it works for donors who respond to statistics and for those who respond to personal stories.

Content Types and Priorities

Launch Content (Required for Go-Live)

Core pages (About, Our Work, Give, Worldwide Directory, Financials), hero stories from mothers (reformatted from existing library), APRN and Option Line impact pages with current statistics, active lawsuit/legal defense content, pastor-specific landing page.

Phase 2 Content (Post-Launch)

Expanded story library with template-driven publishing, downloadable resources for inbound email acquisition (gated content), expanded newsroom with ongoing press release publishing workflow, campaign-specific donation landing pages for email/direct mail integration.

Content Sources

HBI's communications team (Andrea Trudden's department) is the primary content creator, with Mission Advancement handling donor-facing content. Publishing workflow is Google Doc → department lead approval → Comms team proofreading → publish. Three to four team members have publishing access. Content updates on the international site are infrequent — primarily driven by milestones, press releases, and campaign moments rather than ongoing editorial.

SEO and Discoverability

Joe Parker raised AI search optimization as a major strategic consideration. AI crawlers sample roughly the first 10% of a site's pages when the page count is large. The current combined site has over 10,000 pages. Paring heartbeatinternational.org down to only the content that serves its defined purpose means AI crawlers will index a much higher percentage of meaningful content. Schema markup and clean semantic structure will position HBI as an authoritative source for AI-generated answers about the pregnancy help movement. The goal is to become an "answer engine" — not just a website that ranks, but a source that AI systems cite.

Section 06

Copy Seeds

These are directional — specific enough to react to, not final copy. Written in HBI's established voice: inspired, dignifying expertise, relational, benefit-focused, active language.

Home — Hero
"Every heartbeat is a life worth fighting for."

Positioning: Lead with the integrated mission — Reach, Rescue, Renew — as a system, not three separate programs. The annual report and case study HBI is sending show this integrated narrative. Hero should communicate: we're the largest network, we act directly (not just equip), and your support changes outcomes today.

Tone: Confident and visionary. Donor audience needs to feel they're joining a winning movement, not propping up a struggling one.

CTA direction: Dual path — "Give Now" (hard conversion) and "Stay Connected" (email capture, soft conversion). Email capture must be above the fold.

About — What Is a Pregnancy Help Center?
"More than a pregnancy test. A lifeline."

Positioning: This section does critical education work. Many visitors don't understand the distinction between pregnancy centers, adoption agencies, and maternity homes — or why Heartbeat's network includes all three. The copy should dignify the breadth of the movement while making it accessible. Avoid industry jargon; use "pregnancy help organization" as the blanket term per brand standards.

Tone: Trustworthy, experienced, comprehensive. This is the "let me explain" page — authoritative but not clinical.

Option Line — Donor-Facing
"24 hours. 7 days. One call can change everything."

Positioning: Lead with the immediacy and accessibility of the service — real nurses, real conversations, real connections to local help. Pair the data (total contacts served, lives impacted) with a single powerful story. The donor message: your gift keeps this line open.

Key message: Option Line is the bridge between a woman's moment of crisis and the pregnancy help center in her community.

Architecture note: This page tells the donor story of Option Line. It does NOT link to or brand-connect with the client-facing optionline.org experience.

APRN — Donor-Facing
"8,000 lives rescued. And counting."

Positioning: APRN is the most emotionally compelling program for donors — a woman who has taken the first dose of a chemical abortion can still choose life, and the network makes it possible. Lead with the milestone, follow with a story, close with the ask. Use "Abortion Pill Rescue Network" (not "Abortion Pill Reversal") per brand architecture. Refer to "chemical abortions" or "medical abortions" per brand standards.

Tone: Passionate, advocatory, reassuring. This isn't tragedy — it's rescue.

Lawsuits / Legal Defense
"They came for us. We stood our ground."

Positioning: The California and New York AG lawsuits are not just legal news — they're a donor activation engine. Frame as a movement under attack, with Heartbeat leading the defense. This activates the "David vs. Goliath" motivation HBI described. The tone must be confident (not victimized), and the path to giving must be immediate.

Tone: Determined, heartfelt, dignifying. We represent the movement and we stand up for the movement.

Stories of Life Saved
"Her name is [Name]. This is her story."

Positioning: First-person stories from mothers. These exist and are strong — HBI uses them across social, email, and web. The new site needs a clean, emotionally resonant template that lets the story breathe. Each story should end with a clear connection to how the donor's support made it possible.

Tone: Compassionate, genuine, warm. Let the mother's voice lead.

Open Questions

These items were not resolved in Discovery Session 1. Each requires a specific resolution path before design can proceed on the affected area.

Open Item 1 — CMS Selection

The decision to leave Joomla is confirmed. The destination platform is not. HubSpot Content Hub Enterprise was discussed in earlier calls as a possibility. JDA needs to evaluate whether HubSpot CMS, a headless CMS (e.g., Sanity), or another platform best serves HBI's needs — particularly around the Worldwide Directory, preview capability, and the editorial workflow. Resolution: JDA to present CMS recommendation with rationale in a follow-up session. Blocks: page template design, directory implementation approach.

Open Item 2 — Worldwide Directory Implementation

The directory must move from CiviCRM to HubSpot as the data source. The current UX is acknowledged as poor (country selection required, city search unreliable, alphabetical-only results, capped at 5 results). The target UX is "more like optionline.org" — but the technical approach for querying HubSpot data on a public-facing page needs to be defined. Resolution: JDA to scope directory implementation options after CMS decision. Coordinate with Therese on HubSpot data model. Blocks: directory page design, data integration architecture.

Open Item 3 — Option Line / APRN Brand Separation on the Website

Dawn Neville raised an active tension: donors are motivated by Option Line and APRN, but the brand architecture requires clear separation from client-facing experiences. The current approach ("don't say it's our Option Line and our APRN" in donor materials) is described as "squishy" — the team struggles with it because donors respond to the connection. This needs to be revisited with specific recommendations for how the website handles this balance. Resolution: Dawn Neville to lead brand architecture discussion with JDA — how do we tell the donor story of these programs on heartbeatinternational.org without creating an unintended bridge to the client-facing brands? This is a content and design question. Blocks: Option Line page design, APRN page design, navigation language.

Open Item 4 — Donation Platform Integration Details

Donations currently go through an embedded form (likely Virtuous). The new site will embed Virtuous as well, but the specifics of how campaign-specific donation pages work, how the handoff to the Virtuous email cadence is triggered, and whether any of this shifts to HubSpot need to be confirmed. Resolution: Clarify with Andrea Trudden and Therese which donation flows stay in Virtuous vs. migrate to HubSpot. Blocks: donate page template design.

Open Item 5 — Inbound Content / Gated Resources Strategy

HBI mentioned having downloadable resources gated behind email capture for acquisition purposes. The scope, format, and priority of these assets for the new site was not discussed in detail. Resolution: Inventory existing gated content. Determine which assets transfer to the new site and whether new assets are needed for launch. Blocks: resource page design, email integration.

Open Item 6 — Annual Report and Case Study

Andrea committed to sending the newly finalized annual report and case study after the call. These documents illustrate HBI's "integrated system of programs and services" and may inform the site's narrative structure. Resolution: JDA to review upon receipt and incorporate into IA and content planning. Blocks: no hard blocks, but influences overall narrative flow.
